Awe, Pride and Well-Being: Improving Emotion Regulation in Sport and Exercise by Considering Individual and Cultural Determinants

Enno Winkler

2024-09-04

Introduction

First, I will address the central constructs that are reflected in this title. These are awe, pride, their connection to well-being and individual determinants. But first, I want to bring your attention to the background. We see a small section of our neighboring galaxy, and we can see some bright pixels that seem like noise, but they’re not. Each bright pixel is an individual star, and they seem close, but are light years away from each other. Our sun is but one such star. Reflecting on such thing, many people experience awe.

Awe

  • A positive emotion related to (1) vastness and (2) a need for accomodation (Keltner & Haidt, 2003)
    • Transformative (e.g. Chirico et al., 2018)
    • Therapeutic (Dourron et al., 2022; Monroy & Keltner, 2023)

Awe is the emotional reaction to a stimulus that we perceive as vast, such that it challenges the frame of reference that we are used to. This perception of vastness is accompanied by a need for accomodation, that is, the need to re-integrate the self into the new, shaken-up frame of reference. <<<>>> Awe has been labelled a transformative <<<>>> and therapeutic emotion. These properties make it a useful emotion for sport psychologists. Despite this, there is almost no research on awe in sport psychology.

Correlates of Awe

  • Attention: Other-focus (e.g., Bai et al., 2017)
  • Self-Perception: Small self (e.g., Piff et al., 2015)
  • Social Status: Humility (Stellar et al., 2018), selflessness (e.g., Piff et al., 2015)
  • Enhanced meaning in life (Rivera et al., 2019)
  • Enhanced information intake and processing (Danvers & Shiota, 2017)

Awe shifts the attention away from one’s own interests to the outside. <<<>>> Awe makes us feel small. <<<>>> And awe makes individuals more humble, leading to a more balanced view of one’s capabilities. <<<>>> Awe is also linked to the elaboration of meaning in life,<<<>>> and better information intake and processing.

Pride

  • A positive and self-conscious emotion related to socially valued behaviour (Tracy & Robins, 2004)
  • Authentic vs. hubristic pride (Tracy & Robins, 2007)

Pride, on the other hand, is a self-conscious emotion that arises in a context of evaluation by others. Pride facilitates rising in the social hierarchy. There are two ways to rise in the ranks. One is by evaluating oneself as in some way better than others, feeling and displaying dominance, superiority and power. This is associated with hubristic pride. The other route to status is by effort and achievement, which is associated with authentic pride. Sport psychological research views authentic pride as desirable, and hubristic pride as maladaptive and undesirable.

Pride and Training Progress

  • Less authentic pride this week is followed by more training progress next week, but more authentic pride on average is linked to more training progress (Gilchrist et al., 2018)

Together, awe and pride are both positive and arousing, but they seem opposed to one another regarding their appraisal, and that means either pride or awe is high but not both at the same time. And this is interesting because <<<>>> , as Gilchrist and their colleagues found, less authentic pride this week is followed by more training progress next week, but more authentic pride on average is linked to more training progress. Awe also seems particularly opposed to arrogant, hubristic pride, so it might counteract that, too. This combined with the idea that awe might benefit meaningful and realistic goal setting through better information processing and pondering meaning in life makes it a good emotion to have before you start pursuing a goal.

Theory

The Cybernetic Big Five Theory

The Transformation of Characteristic Adaptations in the CB5T, adapted from DeYoung (2015) .

I want to use the Cybernetic Big Five Theory as framework. The Cybernetic Big Five Theory understands individuals as complex self-regulating, a.k.a. cybernetic systems. Just like a thermostat identifies a target temperateure, measures the current temperature and the increases heat to match the target temperature, individuals find goals and deploy strategies to approach their goals. This is represented in the two upper ovals.
Sometimes though, individuals need to adjust their strategies and goals when they encounter some anomalous, previously unknown information, for example when they encounter something too vast to fit in the oval that they are accustomed to. And awe might do just that. Following such disintegration, adapted strategies and goals emerge through reintegration. And after that, pride is an excellent motivator to pursue goals with determination.

Cybernetic Value Fulfillment Theory

Cybernetic Big Five Theory (DeYoung, 2015)

Value Fulfillment Theory (Tiberius, 2018)

Cybernetic Value Fulfillment Theory of Wellbeing (DeYoung & Tiberius, 2022)

There is <<<>>> value fulfillment theory, and there is a combined theory called the cybernetic value fulfillment theory. Grossly oversimplifying, individual and cultural determinants influence the values of people. And, people experience more wellbeing when they are better at (A) setting appropriate goals in line with their values and (B) making progress towards these goals. I think that awe can help people do the former, and pride can help people do the latter.

RQs

  1. How does induced awe affect pride in the context of sport or exercise?
  2. What roles do cultural and individual determinants play?
  3. How does awe induction influence the subsequent experience of pride training or exercise behaviour across weeks?

Methods

Study 1

Flow Diagram for Study 1 - 2x1 Between-Subjects

I want to launch the PhD Project with an experimental study to see how awe affects pride, and how the experience of awe and pride can be predicted by Individual and Cultural Determinants. I assume that awe will shrink pride, especially hubristic pride. To test this, I will use a simple design with two randomized groups of recreational runners that recieve two treatments. After the pre-test, one group will be presented with the definition of awe and then write about an awe experience in exercise, while the other group receives a control treatment. Then, I ask in a questionnaire about their current feelings of awe and pride regarding their training.

Study 2

  1. How does awe induction influence the subsequent experience of pride training or exercise behaviour across weeks?
  • Recreational runners
  • Pre-Test: Individual determinants, well-being
  • Diary (6 training sessions): Awe imagery → pride, awe, affect, goal km, → training → completed km, training time, training intesity → pride statement
  • Post-Test: well-being

In study 2, I want to test my assumptions regarding the adaptive outcomes of a well-timed induction of awe and pride (RQ3). For that, I want to ask runners to fill in a pre-test and then a diary entry before and after every training session. Before a session, I want to have participants listen to guided imagery that induces awe. I can use the open answers from study 1 to tailor such guided imagery to the exercise context. Then, participants fill in their goal and report feelings of pride, awe, and affect. After training, they fill in how long and far they ran, and how intense they ran. With a forced choice from four pride-related statements, I want to encourage participants to feel pride after their training. The induction of awe before and pride after training should help participants to adapt well and pursue their goals more effectively, leading to more well-being after.
