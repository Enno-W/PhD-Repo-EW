Awe, Pride and Well-Being: Improving Emotion Regulation in the Exercise Context by Considering Individual and Cultural Determinants

Enno Winkler

Awe

  • A positive emotion related to (1) vastness and (2) a need for accomodation (Keltner & Haidt, 2003)
    • Transformative (e.g. Chirico et al., 2018)
    • Therapeutic (Dourron et al., 2022; Monroy & Keltner, 2023)

What is awe and why is it important for sport psychology practicioners and athletes to know more about this emotion? Awe is the emotional reaction to a stimulus that we perceive as vast, such that it challenges the frame of reference that we are used to. This stimulus could be, for example, a vast mountain range, but it does not have to be physically large. It can also be a vastly complex stimulus, like intricate artwork, or it could be a profound idea or a vast timescale. This perception of vastness is accompanied by a need for accomodation, that is, the need to re-integrate the self into the new, shaken-up frame of reference. Awe has been labelled a transformative and therapeutic emotion. These properties make it a useful emotion for sport psychologists seeking to bring about a transformation of thought patterns or boosting mental health. Despite this, there is almost no research on awe in sport pscychology beyond the context of extreme sports.

Correlates of Awe

  • Attention: Other-focus (e.g., Bai et al., 2017)
  • Self-Perception: Small self (e.g., Piff et al., 2015)
  • Social Status: Humility (Stellar et al., 2018), selflessness (e.g., Piff et al., 2015)
  • Enhanced meaning in life (Rivera et al., 2019)
  • Creative thinking (Chirico et al., 2018)
  • Enhanced information intake and processing (Danvers & Shiota, 2017)

A growing body of research illuminates how awe relates to other constructs. Regarding attention, awe shifts the attention away from one’s own interests to the outside. Thus, awe is also associated with prosocial tendency. Regarding self-perception, awe makes us feel small. Regarding our status in society, awe makes individuals more humble, leading to a more balanced view of one’s capabilities, and a conscious “leveling” of oneself within the social hierarchy.

Pride

  • A positive and self-conscious emotion related to socially valued behaviour (Tracy & Robins, 2004)
  • Authentic vs. hubristic pride (Tracy & Robins, 2007)

Pride is a self-conscious emotion. That means, it arises exclusively in a context of evaluation by others, as opposed to the basic emotions anger, disgust, fear, happiness, sadness and surprise. Pride facilitates individual rising in the social hierarchy. There are two ways to rise in the ranks. One is by evaluating oneself as in some way better than others, feeling and displaying dominance, superiority and power. This is associated with hubristic pride. The other route to status is by effort and achievement, which is associated with authentic pride. Sport psychological research views authentic pride as desirable, and hubristic pride as maladaptive and undesirable.

Correlates of Pride

Pride in General

  • Attention: Self-focus (Sabiston et al., 2020)

Authentic Pride

  • Humility (Weidman et al., 2018)
  • Social Status: Rise in social status by achievement and effort (Tracy et al., 2020)

Hubristic Pride

  • Self-Perception: Larger, aggrandized self (Tracy et al., 2009)
  • Social Status: Rise in social status by superiority or dominance (Tracy et al., 2020)

The label “self-conscious emotion” gives it away that this is an emotion focused on the self. Interestingly, authentic pride, just like awe, is linked to greater humility. Here, however, the feelings of humility take place in a context of rising in the ranks through achievement and effort, while this is not the case for awe. Hubristic Pride is associated with a larger self, and rising in status with a disregard of others.

To recap, awe and pride are both positive and arousing, but they seem opposed to one another regarding their appraisal. Pride has the core theme of personal achievement, while awe has a core theme of feeling small relative to others.

When we have a broad attentional focus away from the self, we cannot focus on our self at the same time. The self can not be small and large simultaneously. Likewise, when we are selfless and concerned with integrating in existing group structures, we are not concerned with a rise in status. I want to launch the PhD Project with an experimental study on the relationship between awe and pride.
